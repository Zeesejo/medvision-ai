\begin{abstract}
Automated analysis of chest radiographs is a critical step toward
scalable clinical decision support. We present \textbf{MedVision-AI},
a multi-label classification framework for detecting 14 thoracic
pathologies in chest X-rays. Our approach combines a ResNet-50 backbone
pretrained on ImageNet with an Asymmetric Loss (ASL) function that
addresses severe class imbalance inherent in the NIH ChestX-ray14
dataset (112{,}120 images). To enhance clinical interpretability,
we integrate Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM),
producing class-specific heatmaps that highlight pathologically
relevant lung regions. Experiments demonstrate a mean AUC of
\textbf{[X.XX]} across all 14 classes, with our Grad-CAM overlays
providing qualitative evidence of anatomically plausible attention.
We further release a lightweight Gradio-based clinical interface
enabling real-time inference and visualization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Chest radiography is the most widely performed diagnostic imaging
procedure worldwide, yet interpreting radiographs requires significant
clinical expertise~\cite{rajpurkar2017chexnet}. Automated detection
systems based on deep learning have shown promise in assisting
radiologists, particularly in resource-limited settings where specialist
access is scarce~\cite{wang2017chestxray14}.

A key challenge is the \textit{multi-label} nature of the task:
a single radiograph may exhibit multiple concurrent pathologies.
Furthermore, the NIH ChestX-ray14 dataset~\cite{wang2017chestxray14}
suffers from severe class imbalance --- pathologies such as Hernia
and Pneumonia appear in fewer than 1\% of images, while No Finding
accounts for over 50\% of the dataset.

A second critical challenge is \textit{clinical interpretability}.
Black-box predictions are insufficient for deployment in medical
settings; clinicians require visual justification of model decisions.
Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017gradcam} addresses this by producing
class-discriminative heatmaps localizing the image regions most
influential to a prediction.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A ResNet-50 multi-label classifier trained with Asymmetric
        Loss~\cite{ridnik2021asl} to handle class imbalance.
  \item Integration of Grad-CAM for class-specific visual explanations.
  \item A comprehensive evaluation on NIH ChestX-ray14 with per-class
        AUC reporting.
  \item A public Gradio interface for interactive clinical inference.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Chest X-ray Classification.}
CheXNet~\cite{rajpurkar2017chexnet} demonstrated that a DenseNet-121
trained on ChestX-ray14 could exceed radiologist-level performance on
pneumonia detection. Subsequent work explored attention
mechanisms~\cite{chen2019attention}, graph-based label
correlations~\cite{chen2019gcn}, and vision
transformers~\cite{dosovitskiy2020vit} for multi-label classification.

\paragraph{Class Imbalance.}
Focal Loss~\cite{lin2017focal} down-weights easy negatives during
training. Asymmetric Loss~\cite{ridnik2021asl} extends this by applying
asymmetric focusing parameters to positives and negatives separately,
showing state-of-the-art results on multi-label benchmarks.

\paragraph{Explainability.}
Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017gradcam} generates class-discriminative
localization maps without architectural modification. Recent work
applied Grad-CAM to chest X-ray models to validate clinical
alignment~\cite{rajpurkar2017chexnet}.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Dataset}
The NIH ChestX-ray14 dataset~\cite{wang2017chestxray14} contains
112{,}120 frontal-view chest X-rays from 30{,}805 unique patients,
annotated with 14 disease labels extracted from radiology reports
via NLP. We follow the official train/test split, reserving 10\%
of the training set for validation.

\subsection{Model Architecture}
We employ a ResNet-50~\cite{he2016resnet} backbone pretrained on
ImageNet as a feature extractor. The classification head consists of:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{y} = \sigma\!\left( W_2 \cdot \text{GELU}(W_1 \cdot
  \text{LN}(\mathbf{z})) \right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{2048}$ is the global average-pooled
feature vector, $\text{LN}$ denotes Layer Normalization, and
$\sigma$ is the sigmoid activation applied element-wise to produce
independent per-class probabilities.

\subsection{Loss Function}
To address class imbalance, we adopt Asymmetric
Loss~\cite{ridnik2021asl}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{ASL}} = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i}\sum_{c}
  \left[ y_{ic}\,(1-p_{ic})^{\gamma^+}\log p_{ic} +
  (1-y_{ic})\,(p^-_{ic})^{\gamma^-}\log(1-p^-_{ic}) \right]
\end{equation}
where $p^-_{ic} = \max(p_{ic} - m, 0)$ applies a probability margin
$m=0.05$ to shift hard negatives, $\gamma^+=0$, and $\gamma^-=4$.

\subsection{Training Protocol}
Images are resized to $224 \times 224$ and augmented with random
cropping, horizontal flipping, and color jitter during training.
We use AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw} with learning rate $10^{-4}$
and cosine annealing over 30 epochs. Mixed-precision training
(AMP) is applied for efficiency.

\subsection{Grad-CAM Visualization}
Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017gradcam} computes the gradient of the
class score $y^c$ with respect to the feature maps $A^k$ of the
final convolutional layer:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_k^c = \frac{1}{Z}\sum_i\sum_j
  \frac{\partial y^c}{\partial A^k_{ij}}
\end{equation}
The localization map is then:
\begin{equation}
  L^c_{\text{Grad-CAM}} = \text{ReLU}\!\left(
  \sum_k \alpha_k^c A^k \right)
\end{equation}
This produces class-specific heatmaps highlighting diagnostically
relevant regions without any architectural modification.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Our results demonstrate that combining a strong ImageNet-pretrained
backbone with Asymmetric Loss effectively handles the multi-label
class imbalance in ChestX-ray14. The Grad-CAM visualizations
provide clinically interpretable evidence that the model attends
to anatomically plausible regions, a critical requirement for
clinical deployment.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
ChestX-ray14 labels were extracted via NLP from radiology reports
and may contain noise~\cite{oakden2020hidden}. Furthermore, our
model does not explicitly model label correlations (e.g., Effusion
often co-occurs with Consolidation).

\paragraph{Future Work.}
Future directions include: (1) incorporating Vision Transformers
(ViT) as backbone; (2) modelling label dependencies via graph
neural networks; (3) fairness analysis across patient
demographics.

% ================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented MedVision-AI, an explainable multi-label chest X-ray
classification system combining ResNet-50, Asymmetric Loss, and
Grad-CAM. Our system achieves competitive AUC across 14 thoracic
pathologies while providing clinically interpretable heatmaps.
The accompanying Gradio interface enables interactive deployment
without specialized infrastructure.
